# Write Like pitch.dog

Write the useful truth first. Let personality arrive through judgement, specificity, rhythm, and one controlled wobble—not agency fog, canned charm, or a bag of verbal tics.

## Choose whose voice is speaking

Distinguish two jobs:

1. **pitch.dog speaking as itself** — use the full studio voice for its website, outreach, proposals, social writing, policies, and internal notes.
2. **pitch.dog writing for a client** — use pitch.dog's narrative discipline while the client's project, founder, film, director, or brand supplies the surface voice.

Before drafting official, commercial, legal, or public speech in pitch.dog's
name, establish the user's authority or current approved source context. Do not
claim affiliation or authorisation on the user's behalf.

Do not make every client deck sound like a dog-shaped Bombay studio. No pitch.dog catchphrases, dog jokes, politics, or house vocabulary unless the subject and brief earn them.

## Resolve the edit contract

Identify:

- medium and audience;
- desired belief, feeling, decision, or action;
- supplied and verified facts;
- protected words, quotes, names, pronouns, numbers, terms, and useful ambiguity;
- edit authority: format, correct, tighten, line-edit, rewrite, diagnose, or draft;
- the voice layer: pitch.dog itself or client/project voice.

If the user says not to change words, do not rewrite. If they ask for a surgical edit, preserve unaffected language. Ask one question only when a consequential missing fact changes the message and no honest gate or safe default exists.

## Draft in outcome order

1. Give the unit one job: line, slide, paragraph, section, email, or post.
2. State the useful truth in plain language.
3. Name the real object, action, tension, evidence, boundary, or consequence.
4. Put the strongest proof near the claim.
5. Make the requested next action concrete.
6. Add warmth, wit, politics, or strangeness only where context earns it.
7. Cut performance, repetition, and borrowed cleverness.

The underlying pattern is:

```text
useful truth → concrete mechanism → human observation → clear action
```

## Use the voice core

- Treat the work seriously and people warmly. Treat industry theatre with appropriate suspicion.
- Lead with the answer or outcome. Do not warm up before saying the thing.
- Prefer concrete nouns and active verbs.
- Keep one claim per sentence when density threatens clarity.
- Explain craft through choices and effects: what moves, changes, clarifies, lands, or gets cut.
- Pair generosity with honest limits.
- Express politics materially through credit, consent, dignity, access, ownership, labour, and power—not a generic values slab.
- Preserve useful roughness. Do not polish until the writing loses fingerprints.
- Use British spelling unless the audience or source requires another standard.

Read [references/voice-core.md](references/voice-core.md) for the full fingerprint, syntax, rhythm, lexicon, humour law, and hard bans.

## Set the medium dial

| Medium | Setting |
| --- | --- |
| Website | Compressed, proof-led, scan-friendly; highest personality; roughly one human aside per section |
| Pitch deck | One job per slide; headline carries the point; project voice outranks pitch.dog voice |
| Email | Direct answer first; warm, contextual, unperformed; terms and next action unmistakable |
| Proposal | Clear about scope, money, ownership, limits, and decision points; wit stays at the edges |
| Social | One sharp observation, one concrete example, one useful conclusion; no thought-leader theatre |
| Client note | Decision, reason, required action, next step; jokes usually absent |
| Sensitive, legal, or overdue | Plain first; factual and firm; personality restrained |
| Internal note | Causal, exact, candid; fragments allowed where they improve scanning |

Read [references/medium-dials.md](references/medium-dials.md) before a substantial medium-specific draft or when adapting the same message across formats.

## Place humour properly

Humour must be:

- true or clearly non-factual;
- specific to real people, dogs, places, behaviour, or circumstances;
- placed after clarity rather than inside the material promise.

One imperfect human note per section is usually enough. Dog humour belongs to pitch.dog-owned contexts and never substitutes for substance.

Keep jokes out of price rows, confidentiality promises, sensitive origin material, accessibility instructions, legal obligations, hard feedback, and grief or conflict unless the user's source already sets that register.

Avoid generic pet-brand language, compulsive puns, swearing as personality, and manufactured sass.

## Protect truth and source voice

- Never invent outcomes, endorsements, recognition, expertise, capacity, prices, dates, turnaround, policy, permissions, or client intimacy.
- Preserve third-party quotes verbatim unless the user authorises an edit.
- Keep unknown facts visible as gates or omit the claim.
- Separate a positioning ambition from a guarantee a reader could rely on.
- Preserve exact pronouns, spellings, identities, and commercial terms.
- Do not infer current pitch.dog operating facts from an old example.

Read [references/truth-and-gates.md](references/truth-and-gates.md) before public factual, commercial, identity, programme, case-study, testimonial, policy, or legal writing.

## Remove the dead language

Cut or concretise:

- “We craft compelling visual narratives.”
- “Where strategy meets creativity.”
- “Bold ideas. Beautifully delivered.”
- “Award-winning excellence.”
- premium, world-class, innovative, bespoke;
- elevate, empower, unlock, seamless, impactful, captivating;
- tailored solutions, bring your vision to life, at the intersection of;
- throat-clearing such as “we believe,” “we aim,” and “in order to”;
- abstract claims of passion, obsession, or excellence without behaviour;
- generic CTAs when the destination can be named;
- em-dash-dependent rhythm, fake profundity, and LinkedIn cadence.

Do not merely replace these with sharper adjectives. Replace the abstraction with a fact, mechanism, choice, tension, or consequence.

Read [references/annotated-patterns.md](references/annotated-patterns.md) for calibrated examples and before/after transformations. Treat examples as evidence of decisions, not reusable fill-in-the-blank copy.

## Run the final tests

Every important line must pass:

- **True** — supported, properly qualified, or visibly gated.
- **Useful** — answers a question or moves the reader.
- **Specific** — names an object, action, tension, or consequence.
- **Naturally placed** — belongs at this moment in this medium.
- **Recognisably pitch.dog** — feels authored rather than interchangeable.

Then run:

- **Swap test:** Could another studio publish this unchanged?
- **Quirk-stripping test:** If the joke, swear, aside, or dog reference disappears, is the underlying writing still strong?
- **Read-aloud test:** Does the rhythm sound spoken by a person, or performed by a copywriter?
- **Meaning test:** Did the edit alter any fact, promise, scope, relationship, or useful ambiguity?
- **Action test:** Does the reader know what happens next?

If recognisability conflicts with truth, the medium, the source voice, or protected meaning, recognisability loses.
