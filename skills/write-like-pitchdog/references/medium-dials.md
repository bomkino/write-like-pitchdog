# Medium dials

## Contents

- Medium settings
- Website, email, social, client-note, client-deck, long-form, and internal
  guidance
- Surgical edit, voice pass, rewrite, and diagnose modes

Apply the voice at different strengths. Do not make every surface sound like a
website headline.

| Medium | Brand personality | Humour | Detail | Primary job |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---|
| Studio website | High | Selective | Medium | Orient, prove, answer, move |
| Studio social | High | Selective | Low–medium | Share one useful observation |
| Studio email | Medium | Low | As needed | Answer and advance the work |
| Client note or handover | Low | Rare | High | Record decisions and next actions |
| Client deck | Client-led | Usually none | Controlled | Make the client's argument land |
| Long-form studio note | Medium–high | Selective | High | Teach through a clear sequence |
| Internal note | Candid | Natural | Low–high | Think and decide without performance |

## Studio website

- Put identity and offer first; show work before long explanation.
- Give each section one claim.
- Use an orientation label, a claim-led heading, a specific explanation, proof
  or details, and a named action.
- Keep headings short enough to scan on a phone.
- Let one human aside puncture polish; skip it when the section already has
  enough personality.
- Start FAQ answers with yes, no, sometimes, or the actual decision.
- Use outcome language without guaranteeing an outcome.
- A call to action names what happens: see the work, send the brief, build an
  estimate, book a call.

## Studio email

- Lead with the answer, update, amount, request, or decision.
- Preserve the sender's original language when editing. Make surgical changes
  unless a rewrite is requested.
- Use a short paragraph for the human context, then bullets only when they make
  scope or next steps easier to verify.
- State money, dates, deliverables, and dependencies plainly.
- End with one clear action or question.
- Keep humour out of billing disputes, legal issues, complaints, and sensitive
  feedback. With an established client, one natural aside is enough.
- Do not turn an operational email into brand copy.

## Studio social

- Open with a concrete observation, not engagement bait.
- Show one real example or decision.
- Explain why it changes the work.
- End on the useful conclusion. Do not force a question for comments.
- Pride in the work is welcome; chest-beating and invented universality are not.
- Avoid LinkedIn cadence: stacked one-line revelations, false suspense,
  motivational uplift, and “here are three lessons” unless there are genuinely
  three useful lessons.
- Use dog language only when the dogs or studio life are actually part of the
  post.

## Client note, feedback, or handover

Use this order:

1. What changed or what was reviewed.
2. Why the decision helps the work.
3. What remains open.
4. What the client needs to do.
5. What happens next, with a date if known.

Keep the tone warm and unambiguous. Separate taste from functional failure.
Quote the exact unresolved wording when precision matters. Do not hide a hard
decision under praise.

## Client deck

The project voice outranks the studio voice.

- Define the audience, room, decision, and desired after-effect.
- Give each slide one narrative move.
- Write claim-led headlines; avoid labels such as “Market” when the headline can
  state what matters about the market.
- Use detail as proof after attention exists.
- Control what becomes visible and when.
- Match genre, character, founder, director, and source language.
- Preserve useful ambiguity in creative material.
- Never add pitch.dog jokes or house phrases merely to make the deck sound
  “written.”
- Do not invent evidence, outcomes, customer language, or financial logic.

## Long-form studio note

- Begin with a precise contention.
- Develop one idea through examples, consequences, and a practical test.
- Use subheads that advance the argument.
- Prefer a worked observation over a list of generic tips.
- Keep the prose calmer than the website. The reader has already granted more
  attention.
- Treat any sample article from the source handover as structural evidence, not
  approved copy to reproduce.

## Internal note

- Be candid and concrete.
- Fragments, shorthand, and swearing may remain when they are natural to the
  author; do not add them to perform informality.
- Distinguish fact, inference, judgement, and unresolved choice.
- Record the decision and its reason so the note remains useful later.

## Edit modes

### Surgical edit

Retain vocabulary, rhythm, and structure. Fix only the requested grammar,
clarity, formatting, or factual issue. Do not replace good roughness with
polish.

### Voice pass

Keep the meaning and facts. Remove generic phrasing, strengthen the message
spine, and restore natural pitch.dog rhythm.

### Rewrite

Rebuild structure when the original does not serve the audience or action.
Preserve any exact user language worth keeping and disclose material changes
when useful.

### Diagnose

Identify:

- the actual job of the copy;
- lines that could belong to any studio;
- unsupported claims;
- missing evidence or permission;
- overdone rhythm or humour;
- the smallest useful fix.
