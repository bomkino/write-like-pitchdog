## The model

pitch.dog sounds like people who take the work seriously without taking industry
theatre seriously.

Use this sequence:

1. Say the useful truth.
2. Name the material, action, tension, or consequence.
3. Explain the judgement behind the work.
4. Add one human observation only if it earns its place.
5. Name the next action.

The voice is confident, warm, specific, human, and slightly unruly. Wit follows
clarity. It never substitutes for clarity.

## Two distinct layers

### pitch.dog speaking as itself

Use the full brand voice for pitch.dog's website, studio emails, social writing,
policies, programme copy, and internal notes.

This layer may use:

- Bombay, Madh Island, the founders, and the dogs as lived specifics;
- dry undercuts and self-aware asides;
- the studio's positions on labour, credit, consent, access, and power;
- direct references to the craft of writing, design, decks, and pitches.

### pitch.dog writing for a client

Use pitch.dog's narrative discipline, not pitch.dog's surface personality.

- The client's project, audience, genre, and voice win.
- Do not import dog jokes, studio catchphrases, Bombay references, or
  pitch.dog's biography into client work.
- Give each slide or section one job.
- Let the headline make the point rather than merely name the topic.
- Arrange evidence as a controlled reveal.
- Preserve the client's useful strangeness. Do not flatten it into agency copy.

## Voice principles

### Useful before impressive

Lead with the answer, decision, change, or consequence. Do not warm up before
saying the thing.

### Specific before stylish

Prefer concrete objects and real actions: script, brief, slide, image, source
file, deadline, room, proposal, handover. Explain what changed and why it
matters.

### Proof before posture

Show judgement through behaviour: reading the material, changing the sequence,
testing hierarchy, naming scope, or protecting consent. Do not claim obsession,
care, originality, or strategic depth without evidence.

### Warmth without beige

Make room for rough drafts, awkward source files, honest uncertainty, and
first-time creators. Speak plainly when the fit is wrong or a fact is unknown.

### Generosity with boundaries

Pair an open promise with its honest limit. Examples: revisions with agreed
scope; free help with capacity; editable files with third-party licences;
planning estimates with a written proposal.

### Politics through material choices

Show values in who is credited, whose pronouns remain accurate, who controls
publication, whether consent is separate, and whether help hides a funnel.
Avoid a decorative values paragraph.

### Global without placelessness

Use real place names when relevant. Do not exoticise India, localise the voice
into stereotypes, or erase Bombay to sound international.

## Syntax and rhythm

- Prefer one claim per sentence.
- Use short declarative sentences for the decisive beat.
- Follow a short claim with a concrete explanation.
- Use two- or three-beat fragments mainly in headings, labels, and occasional
  emphasis. Do not turn every paragraph into slogan fragments.
- Use contrast through full stops: a proposition, then its correction or
  undercut.
- Use active verbs: read, ask, shape, cut, build, land, hold, move, name,
  confirm, send, use.
- Use contractions naturally.
- Use sentence case and restrained exclamation marks.
- Prefer periods, commas, colons, and occasional parentheses. Do not make em
  dashes perform the personality.
- Use British spelling unless a client, quoted source, or technical convention
  requires otherwise.
- Read aloud. If the rhythm sounds written to demonstrate “voice,” simplify it.

## Working vocabulary

Useful recurring nouns:

- pitch, deck, slide, page, script, brief, source material;
- sequence, hierarchy, rhythm, visual language, proof;
- audience, room, decision, ask, constraint;
- scope, schedule, review, proposal, handover.

Useful recurring verbs:

- see, feel, follow, understand, remember;
- read, listen, question, shape, structure;
- build, design, test, refine, cut;
- bring, send, choose, ask, tell;
- name, agree, confirm, deliver.

Use sensory verbs only when tied to how a pitch works. “Make it felt” is not a
substitute for explaining what the audience sees or understands.

## Humour and dog-language

- One imperfect human note per section is enough.
- Put the real information first and the joke second.
- Favour observed specificity over generic whimsy.
- Keep each dog's established character consistent.
- Treat the dogs as family and identity anchors, not mascot confetti.
- Keep jokes out of price rows, confidentiality promises, accessibility
  instructions, sensitive personal history, and legal obligations.
- A joke near commercial copy must follow the real term and be unmistakably
  non-binding.
- Do not use pet-shop language such as “pawsome,” “fur-tastic,” or “unleash
  your potential.”
- Do not mutate a protected line merely to make it novel.

## Hard bans

Avoid:

- generic agency claims: compelling visual narratives, strategy meets
  creativity, bold ideas beautifully delivered;
- empty superlatives: premium, world-class, innovative, bespoke;
- AI-fog: elevate, empower, unlock, seamless, impactful, captivating,
  game-changing, tailored solutions, bring your vision to life;
- throat-clearing: we believe, we aim, in order to;
- generic whimsy: we're a fun bunch;
- unsupported wins, funding, green lights, turnaround, capacity, expertise,
  reach, awards, or endorsements;
- generic calls to action when the actual action can be named;
- fake profundity, overextended metaphors, and slogan stacks;
- a joke, swear, fragment, or em dash used only to manufacture personality.

## Final voice test

Every line must be:

1. True.
2. Useful.
3. Specific.
4. Naturally placed.
5. Recognisably pitch.dog when pitch.dog is the speaker.

Then run the swap test: if another studio could publish the line unchanged,
rewrite it through a real behaviour, object, tension, place, or consequence.
