# Annotated patterns

## Contents

- Small calibration corpus

- Failure examples and calibration test

These lines calibrate judgement. Do not recycle them automatically. A voice
made from repeated catchphrases becomes a costume.

## Small calibration corpus

### “Every slide has one job.”

Why it works: one concrete object, one operating rule, no decoration.

### “A slide has one moment.”

Why it works: compresses a sequencing principle into a line the following copy
can prove.

### “Same material. Clearer pitch.”

Why it works: a compact before-and-after claim without pretending the studio
invented the underlying idea.

### “Small keeps the work close.”

Why it works: turns studio size into a client-relevant consequence rather than
a lifestyle claim.

### “Useful beats polished.”

Why it works: states a working preference in three ordinary words.

### “Real notes. Not fluff.”

Why it works: promise and boundary. The surrounding copy must then show what
useful feedback contains.

### “Bring us the unfinished thought.”

Why it works: welcoming without generic reassurance; it names what a hesitant
client may actually have.

### “Help should not always be a funnel.”

Why it works: political position expressed through a material business
practice.

### “No templates. The visual language is built around your material.”

Why it works: the negative claim is immediately followed by the method.

### “No. It is a useful working range.”

Why it works: direct answer first, qualification second.

## Before-and-after patterns

The left side names the failure mode. The right side shows the stronger move.

| Generic move | pitch.dog move | Why |
|---|---|---|
| “We obsess so you don't have to.” | “We learn the project before we style it.” | Replace self-praise with behaviour. |
| “Every great deck starts with a conversation.” | “Bring us the unfinished thought.” | Replace a category cliché with the reader's real situation. |
| “However you prefer.” | “Call or email. Both reach us.” | Name the actual options. |
| “We'd rather be small and good than big and busy.” | “Small keeps the work close.” | State the consequence, not the self-image. |
| “Same content, but more engaging.” | “The hierarchy changed, the noise left, and the idea became easier to follow.” | Explain the mechanism. |
| “We deliver strategic, polished presentations.” | “We question sequence, emphasis, and pacing so the story lands.” | Replace adjectives with judgement. |
| “Our process is collaborative and transparent.” | “The proposal names scope, schedule, feedback rhythm, and exclusions.” | Show what transparency consists of. |
| “We work with clients around the world.” | Name the verified reach and explain how time zones or review rhythms work. | Turn scale into a useful fact. |

## Reusable rhetorical shapes

Use these shapes, not the source wording.

### Claim → mechanism

> Short claim. Then explain the choices, actions, or evidence that make it true.

### Invitation → useful minimum

> Welcome rough material. Then name the smallest information needed to respond
> well.

### Generosity → boundary

> State the generous policy. Then name scope, capacity, rights, or behaviour
> that keeps it workable.

### No → alternative

> Answer no plainly. Then offer the truthful adjacent option when one exists.

### Expected objection → dry undercut

> Name the concern. Answer it seriously. Add one short human beat only after
> the answer is secure.

### Abstract value → operating evidence

> Do not announce empathy, inclusion, or care. Show who controls consent, whose
> work is credited, and how people are treated.

## Failure examples

Rewrite lines that rely on:

- “compelling,” “impactful,” “bespoke,” or “world-class” instead of evidence;
- “we believe” before a claim that could simply be stated;
- three metaphors where one concrete noun would work;
- a generic dog pun unrelated to the content;
- a guarantee disguised as confidence;
- an em dash or swear as the only sign of personality;
- a string of one-line paragraphs designed to simulate profundity.

## Calibration test

Given a draft, ask:

1. What real object or decision is this sentence about?
2. What does the reader understand or do after it?
3. What evidence makes the claim safe?
4. Is the human beat specific to this studio or project?
5. Would deleting the cleverest phrase make the line clearer?

If the last answer is yes, delete it.
